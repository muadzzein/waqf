Inheritance in Islam

Inheritance is the entry of living persons into possession of dead persons' property and exists in some form wherever the institution of private property is recognised as the basis of the social and economic system. The actual forms of inheritance and the laws governing it, however, differ according to the ideals of different societies.

Main Considerations in Islamic Law of Inheritance

  • To break up the concentration of wealth in individuals and spread it out in society.
  • To respect the property right of ownership of an individual earned through honest means.
  • To hammer in the consciousness of man the fact that man is not the absolute master of wealth he produces but he is its trustee and is not, therefore, authorised to pass it on to others as he likes.
  • To consolidate the family system which is the social unit of an Islamic society.
  • To give incentive to work and encourage economic activity as sanctioned by Islam.

Evils in Pre-Islamic and Modern Laws of Inheritance

  • Women had been completely denied the share of inheritance.
  • In tribal societies, even weak and sick persons and minor children were given no share in inheritance.
  • Primogeniture, which entitles only the eldest son to inherit the whole of the father's property or to get the lion's share.

Reforms Introduced by Islam in the Law of Inheritance

  • Defined and determined the share of each inheritor and imposed limits on the right of the property-owner to dispose of his property according to his whim and caprice.
  • Made women co-sharers with men and restored their dignity and safeguarded their social and economic rights.
  • Laid rules for the break-up of concentrated wealth and helped in its proper and equitable distribution.
  • Eliminated the law of primogeniture and provided a democratic basis for the division of the property of the deceased.

Islamic Rules for Distribution of Inheritance

The property, both movable and immovable, can be distributed after meeting the following obligations:

  1. Funeral expenses.
  2. Clearing off the debts incurred by the deceased.
  3. Payment of bequest, if any, to the extent of one-third of the total assets.

Four persons cannot get inheritance:

  1. A fugitive slave who has fled away from his master.
  2. One who has murdered one's predecessor intentionally or unintentionally.
  3. One who professes a religion other than Islam.
  4. One living in Dar-ul-Harb cannot inherit the property of one living in Dar-ul-Islam and vice versa.

Heirs and Their Shares in Islamic Law of Inheritance

A. Dhaw-u'l-Fara'id (Sharers with Definite Shares)

These sharers are twelve in number:

  • Four males: father, grandfather, uterine brothers, and husband.
  • Eight females: wife, single daughter, son's daughter, mother, grandmother, full sister, consanguine sister, uterine sister.

Share of some of the sharers:

  • Father's share is one-sixth when the deceased leaves a son or a son's son.
  • Grandfather's share is like that of father's share but with certain conditions.
  • Mother is entitled to one-sixth when she has a child or grandchild.
  • Real daughter gets one-half when alone, and two-thirds if more than one.
  • Full sister gets one-half if alone, and two-thirds if more than one.
  • Mother is entitled to one-third of the share if childless.

B. 'Asaba (Residuary Heirs)

'Asaba implies those relatives in whose line of relationship no female enters.

Order of succession:

  • Son: He is the first to get the residue. Daughters are entitled to half of the share as given to the son.
  • Father, grandfather, and great-grandfather fall under the category of 'Asaba in certain situations.
  • Brother and his son, grandson, or consanguine brother.
  • Full paternal uncle.

C. Dhaw-u'l Arham (Relations Connected through Females)

These relatives rarely get any share in the inheritance.

  • Son of the daughter and daughter of the daughter.
  • Son of the daughter of the son, daughter of the daughter of the son, and their children.
  • Maternal grandfather, maternal grandfather of the father, the grandfather of the mother, the maternal grandfather of the mother, the grandmother of the mother, the children of the sisters, the sisters of the father, and those of the mother, etc.
